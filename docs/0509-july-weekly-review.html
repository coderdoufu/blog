Dear investors,

This weekly update will explain why I have kept some of my investments in red and why I will remain committed to them.

Ping An Insurance#

Ping An Insurance is one of my top three holdings. It is one of the stocks that I own most before Covid-19. According to my previous posts, I am more inclined to invest in Chinese stocks as the majority of them have a good valuation and their profits and revenues seem to justify it fairly. Ping An is China's largest insurance company. The company's business model is supported by two methods of growth: A) Finance + Technology; and B) Finance + Ecosystem. Each of the growth models is supported by a number of pillars. In growth model A, it is focused on asset management, finance, and insurance. As for growth model B, it has various ecosystems such as financial services, healthcare, auto services, and smart cities.

Many businesses around the world were negatively affected by the pandemic last year. China is no exception. Ping An, despite economic instability, posted a positive growth number for its group operating profit. In spite of a 2.3% decline in the first half of 2020, retail operating profit is still up 0.1% in comparison with the same period last year.

Why Ping An Insurance price fall?#

Why did the price of Ping An Insurance fall? There are a few reasons why Ping An share price has been going downwards recently.

  1. China Copower is rumored to be selling out its Ping An shares. Securities Market Weekly hailed Li Chi, founder of Shenzhen Co-power Investment Management Company, as one of the most successful investors in China for inheriting the 'Buffett' investment approach. Additionally, he is a diehard Ping An fan and earned a healthy return when the company's share price was in the single digits.

  2. As the number of insurance agents continues to decline, most investors have fled the market. Due to the pandemic, a drop has been observed because a lot of middle-class people cannot afford insurance (in China, many people do not trust insurance, believing it cannot protect them and their assets). The perception of the insurance industry in China is likely to remain the same even after the pandemic has ended.

  3. CapitaLand's stake purchase was expensive. Ping An acquired stakes in Raffles City Shanghai, Raffles City Beijing, Raffles City Ningbo, Raffles City Chengdu, Raffles City Changning (Shanghai) and Raffles City Hangzhou from CaptiLand. This purchase has drawn the ire of many investors who believe that it is too expensive, but Ping An has replied that it will help to increase insurance fund appreciation.

  4. Its first-quarter profits were hurt by exposure to a troubled property developer. With greater scrutiny over China's property sector from Beijing, which has sought to control leverage, China Fortune Land Development defaulted on $530 million in dollar debt in March of this year. Post-tax, Ping An's Fortune Land adjustments amounted to Rmb10bn in the first quarter.

Why am I still holding Ping An?#

These reasons accounted for most of its share price movements:

  1. Many foreign investors have been scared off by China's antitrust policy,
  2. Ping An's investors started to wonder why the company is not buying back its shares (a reduction in the number of outstanding shares often leads to a price increase, thus supporting its current value and preventing further price declines).
  3. Reforms at Ping An Life raised questions among investors.

China stocks, as I always said, are always closely correlated with the Chinese government's policies. Obviously, this is something we cannot avoid and it has less impact on Ping An since its majority business happens in China and it does not own any assets that might raise concerns about antitrust. Shares of Ping An have been repurchased several times in 2019 and 2020 at prices of around 80 RMB per share. A share buyback program has not been announced by the company, however. Particularly in this moment, I believe this is crucial to regaining the trust of the market. In spite of the lack of a buyback, the companies are still making money. The recent earnings report of Ping An Life shows some positive results with regard to its reform. In any case, there is still a year to wait before we see the effects of the reform, according to the analyst.

There has been an increase in foreign investments in Ping An and a reduction in local investments. Due to the focus of local funds on Ping An's short-term growth, its share price is expected to stay around the HKD70 - HKD80 range in the short term. In the future, analysts expect profits from Ping An's life insurance reform to produce profits. Studies provided by many analysts indicate that foreign funds usually focus on the long-term growth of a company.

China still lag behind many countries in terms of insurance penetration

Overall, I still think that Ping An is a good investment in the long run, given its current share price levels.
