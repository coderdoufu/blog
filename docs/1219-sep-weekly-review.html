Greetings, investors

My portfolio underwent a few changes during this month, so I thought I'd give a short update.

In this post, I would like to address two important questions:

  1. What changes have I made to my portfolio?
  2. What happened to Ping An's share price? Is the debt crisis of Chinese developer Evergrande likely to affect it?

How has my portfolio been?#

Matic coin's stop-loss limit was triggered by a sell-off on 7th September that occurred after El Salvador became the first country to adopt Bitcoin as legal tender. Despite the lack of news, this sell-off was triggered by massive profit-taking, which reflects a traditional trend from the market to sell the news. The price promptly bounced back after a strong signal, so I bought the coin back immediately. Xinyi Solar and JD.com, two companies I own on Chinese stock exchanges, made some profits when I sold them. Despite my confidence in these two companies, I would have to shift more funds to crypto after a confirmed uptrend is shown on the Bitcoin chart. Further, I completely sold the Discovery Communications stocks and took profits from Meli as well. As a result, we moved the cash into Bitcoin and Ada.

Can you tell me what happened to Ping An?#

Source: https://i.ytimg.com/vi/uBU9i4VcQe4/maxresdefault.jpg

Having covered Ping An in a comprehensive review two weeks ago, many people might wonder what the impact of Evergrande's debt crisis would be on Ping An's future value. As we are all aware, Ping An is one of the most significant Chinese property 'investors'. According to Xie Yonglin, Ping An's co-chief executive officer, the company's primary purpose for investing in property is to generate rental income rather than to speculate on land. In contrast to Evergrande, which is a Chinese property 'developer', the government of China will impose significant regulatory burdens on the company. Although this is true, Ping An is still subject to regulatory risks. Ping An's biggest investment, China Fortune Land, was the first victim of the "Three Red Lines" in 2021, costing Ping An $3.2 billion in net profit in the first six months. By setting ceilings on the debt-to-asset ratio, net debt-to-equity ratio, and short-term borrowing-to-cash reserves ratio, the "three red lines" framework prevents banks from providing financing.

In terms of Ping An's property investment profile, we would find that it has '0% exposure' to most problematic property developers in China, such as Evergrande and Oceanwide Holdings. Companies that have been invested in by the company, such as Country Garden, China Jinmao, and CIFI Group respect the new policy of three red lines and have competent financial records. In terms of real estate investments, it accounts for less than 5% of its total insurance funds, which is again much lower than the 30% limit set by the Chinese authorities (the real estate investment of an insurance fund cannot exceed 30%).

Despite Ping An's share price continuing to be affected by the news, I am not overly concerned about the company here. The fundamentals of this stock remain solid, and it has gotten cheaper and cheaper to hold. As soon as the technical chart shows a more clear signal, I will consider increasing my holdings.
